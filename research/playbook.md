# YouTube Content Strategy Playbook for B2B SaaS

**Author:** Jesslyn
**Based on:** research from `/research/sources.md` and `/research/youtube-transcripts/`
**Last updated:** April 2026

---

## Introduction

This playbook is a practical guide for B2B SaaS companies that want to use YouTube as a main content and growth channel. It's built from research across 5 expert transcripts and 10 sources, with my own opinions on what actually makes sense to apply in 2026.

I believe YouTube is still one of the most impactful platforms in the world, even with short-form video on the rise. YouTube lets you go deep — and depth is what B2B buyers actually need before they decide to buy something. The experts I trust most in this research have built real companies using these strategies, not just talked about them.

## Part 1: Core Strategy

### Step 1 — Know exactly who you're talking to before you make anything

Before you record a single video, get really clear on who your audience is. What problems do they have? What do they search for? What stresses them out?

Every video topic, thumbnail, hook, and CTA should be made for that specific person — not just for general views.

> "Step zero is always ICP and customer research. That will be crucial and will determine everything else we do." — Sam Dunning, Breaking B2B ([youtube.com/@BreakingB2B](https://www.youtube.com/@BreakingB2B))

### Step 2 — Build your personal brand as a founder first

The fastest way to grow a B2B SaaS YouTube channel isn't production quality — it's being real and authentic. Be clear about who you are, stay consistent, and be honest in ways that big companies can't be.

> "My LinkedIn audience allowed me to scale RB2B from zero to $1M ARR in just six weeks. Content is the ultimate leverage." — Adam Robinson, RB2B ([youtube.com/@rb2b](https://www.youtube.com/@rb2b))

Share your failures, not just your wins. The most engaged audiences come from honesty, not from looking perfect.

### Step 3 — Make videos for every stage of the buyer journey

Not all videos serve the same purpose. Here's how to think about it:

**Top of funnel (8–15 mins):** Easy educational videos. Help people understand a problem. Attract a wide audience.

**Middle of funnel (15–30 mins):** Deeper content. For people who are already interested in what you offer.

**Bottom of funnel:** Case studies, client interviews, product walkthroughs. These push someone from "interested" to "ready to buy."

> "Top of funnel videos attract a lot of your ICP. Middle of funnel is for people further down. Bottom of funnel is case studies and client interviews — that last push." — Sam Dunning, Breaking B2B ([youtube.com/@BreakingB2B](https://www.youtube.com/@BreakingB2B))

### Step 4 — Match your content to where your buyer is mentally

Not every viewer is at the same stage of awareness. Here's a simple breakdown:

| Stage | What they know | What content to make |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Don't know they have a problem | Broad educational YouTube videos |
| 2 | Know they have a problem | How-to guides, tutorials |
| 3 | Know solutions exist | Comparisons, "best X software" content |
| 4 | Know your product | Versus pages, case studies |
| 5 | Ready to buy | Long demos, features, security info |

> "Start at the bottom. Create Stage 5 content first because it helps close sales immediately, then work your way up." — Rob Walling, MicroConf ([youtube.com/@MicroConf](https://www.youtube.com/@MicroConf))

### Step 5 — Put two CTAs in every video

Don't wait until the end to ask people to take action. Use two CTAs:

- **CTA 1 at around the 33% mark:** Short and soft. For people who are already warm and ready.
- **CTA 2 at the end:** More direct. After you've given them value and built trust.

> "Two CTAs per video — one at 33% mark, one at the end. This is the highest converting version we have found." — Sam Dunning, Breaking B2B ([youtube.com/@BreakingB2B](https://www.youtube.com/@BreakingB2B))

### Step 6 — Put real effort into your thumbnails

Your thumbnail is 60–70% of how your video performs. Simple rules:

- Max 4 words
- Contrasting colors
- Big face (yours, the person actually in the video)
- Simple enough to understand in one glance

> "Thumbnail is around 60–70% of the performance right now on YouTube." — Sam Dunning, Breaking B2B ([youtube.com/@BreakingB2B](https://www.youtube.com/@BreakingB2B))

### Step 7 — Push your videos across all your other channels

YouTube videos are your strongest content asset. Don't let them sit there — push them everywhere:

- Write a LinkedIn post for every video you publish
- Mention new videos in your email newsletter
- Send relevant videos to prospects before sales calls to warm them up

> "Long-form YouTube videos will be more powerful than any LinkedIn post you make or newsletter you write. Push it out to as many other channels as possible." — Sam Dunning, Breaking B2B ([youtube.com/@BreakingB2B](https://www.youtube.com/@BreakingB2B))

## Part 2: Where Experts Disagree

### Disagreement 1: Strict step-by-step model vs. flexible hybrid approach

**Simon Høiberg** recommends a very structured acquisition model: first get 5 users manually, then use referrals to get to 100, then switch to content and YouTube, then add paid ads. Each stage has to be done in order.
([youtube.com/@SimonHoiberg](https://www.youtube.com/@SimonHoiberg))

**Adam Robinson** does the opposite: combine content and outreach from day one. Drive traffic through content, identify warm visitors, and reach out to them immediately. He scaled RB2B to $1M ARR in 6 weeks with this approach.
([youtube.com/@rb2b](https://www.youtube.com/@rb2b))

**My take: Adam Robinson's approach.** Simon's model is too rigid for 2026. You don't have to "unlock" the next stage before starting the next one. Doing content and outreach at the same time builds trust faster and gets results quicker. The linear approach leaves a lot of compounding growth on the table.

### Disagreement 2: Start from top of funnel vs. start from bottom of funnel

**Sam Dunning** focuses on building broad top-of-funnel content first to attract a wide audience, then bringing them down.

**Rob Walling** strongly disagrees: start from the bottom (Stage 5 — people who are almost ready to buy) and work upward. Bottom-of-funnel content closes deals right away and also works as sales material.
([youtube.com/@MicroConf](https://www.youtube.com/@MicroConf))

**My take: Rob Walling's approach.** Especially for early-stage companies, it makes more sense to create content that helps close existing deals first. Top-of-funnel content takes months to convert — that's a long time to wait if you need revenue now.

### Disagreement 3: Optimize for views vs. optimize for leads

**Dan Martell** focuses on differentiation and positioning — if you stand out enough, revenue and leads will follow naturally.
([youtube.com/@DanMartell](https://www.youtube.com/@DanMartell))

**Sam Dunning** is more direct: views and subscribers are vanity metrics. The only thing that matters is lead quality and quantity. Broad videos that get views but bring in the wrong audience are a waste of time.
([youtube.com/@BreakingB2B](https://www.youtube.com/@BreakingB2B))

**My take: Sam Dunning for B2B specifically.** In B2B SaaS, your audience is narrow by definition. 100,000 views from the wrong people does nothing. 500 views from your exact ICP is worth way more.

## Part 3: What I Rejected and Why

### Rejected 1: Breaking B2B's framework as a complete standalone strategy

Sam Dunning's framework is useful for some things — CTA placement, thumbnail rules, video structure. But as a full strategy, it's too general. It covers broad principles without going deep enough into what actually makes B2B SaaS YouTube content convert. It also doesn't talk about the shift toward shorter attention spans at all, which feels like a big gap in 2026.

A lot of his advice applies to any YouTube channel, not specifically B2B SaaS. I kept the tactical elements but didn't use it as the main strategic framework.

### Rejected 2: Simon Høiberg's strict step-by-step acquisition model

Simon's model ([youtube.com/watch/6P_H9eDNBcA](https://www.youtube.com/watch/6P_H9eDNBcA)) treats user acquisition as something you have to do in exact order. In real life, this doesn't work. You can build your YouTube presence while doing manual outreach at the same time — they don't block each other. Waiting until you hit 100 referral users before starting content means you're losing months of growth for no good reason.

The model also treats YouTube and short-form video as completely separate, when combining them from the start would actually speed things up.

## Part 4: My Original Ideas

### Idea: The "Gateway Video" — Under 5 Minutes for New Audiences

Every expert in this research recommends long-form YouTube content (at least 8 minutes). But in 2026, attention is short. A lot of potential B2B buyers first find you through short-form content on TikTok, Reels, or YouTube Shorts.

My idea is a **"gateway video" format** — videos under 5 minutes that cover one useful concept completely. Not a teaser or a clip, but a real standalone piece that gives value in under 5 minutes, then links to a longer video for people who want to go deeper.

This bridges the gap between short-form and long-form without abandoning either. It meets people where they are (short attention spans, scrolling on mobile) while still using YouTube as your main depth platform. Under-5-minute videos are also easier to share on LinkedIn, which feeds back into your whole distribution system.

None of the experts I researched specifically suggested this. They treat short-form and long-form as two completely separate strategies — but I think combining them as a funnel within YouTube itself is a real opportunity that's being missed.

## Part 5: Weaknesses of This Playbook

**1. LinkedIn data is incomplete.**
LinkedIn posts were only collected from 5 out of 10 experts. The other 5 experts' LinkedIn presence wasn't analyzed, so some perspectives on how they distribute YouTube content on LinkedIn might be missing.

**2. This playbook assumes a founder is the face of the content.**
A lot of this advice is built around founder-led content. For companies where the founder doesn't want to be on camera, or where there are multiple founders, this needs to be adapted significantly.

**3. None of this has been personally tested.**
Everything here is based on what other people say worked for them. What works for a $35M ARR company (Adam Robinson) might not work the same way for a new startup. That gap is real.

**4. The "gateway video" idea is unproven.**
My original idea in Part 4 is based on observation and logic, not data. It hasn't been tested in a real B2B SaaS context and could fail if the audience doesn't engage with short YouTube content the same way they do with Reels or TikTok.

**5. The space is getting crowded.**
Multiple experts point out that YouTube for B2B is becoming more competitive. This playbook doesn't go deep into how to actually stand out in a saturated niche — which is honestly one of the hardest parts.

## Part 6: Who I Would NOT Recommend Following and Why

### Y Combinator ([youtube.com/@ycombinator](https://www.youtube.com/@ycombinator))

Y Combinator makes good content, but it's not the right source for YouTube content strategy. Their videos are aimed at founders building venture-backed startups — the advice is mostly about fundraising, scaling fast, and hitting unicorn-level metrics. That's a very different context from most B2B SaaS companies, especially bootstrapped ones.

The transcript I collected was a founder story about building a $2B company. It was interesting, but there was almost no actionable insight about YouTube content strategy specifically. Following YC for YouTube advice would send most B2B SaaS founders down a path that doesn't apply to their actual situation.

For YouTube content strategy, YC should be replaced with people who are actively running content-driven B2B SaaS channels today — like Adam Robinson or Sam Dunning.
